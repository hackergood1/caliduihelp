What Happens If You Get a DUI in California?

Last updated: April 2026

When someone gets pulled over and arrested for DUI, two separate things start at the same time: a criminal case in court, and a DMV case about your license. These are completely independent — you can actually win one and lose the other.

The Court Process (Step by Step)

  1. Arrest & booking — You might get held for a few hours or overnight.
  2. Arraignment — Your first court appearance. You enter a plea: guilty, not guilty, or no contest.
  3. Pre-trial / negotiation — This is where a lawyer might try to reduce the charge (like to a "wet reckless").
  4. Trial — Most cases don't make it here. They usually get resolved before this.

The DMV Side (This Catches People Off Guard)

After a DUI arrest, you typically have 10 days to request a DMV hearing. If you don't:

Your license automatically gets suspended — even if you haven't gone to court yet.

The DMV case moves completely independently from your criminal case. Don't ignore it.

What the Penalties Actually Feel Like

Jail Time

  • First offense: usually little to none — often probation instead
  • Second/third offense: way more likely to serve actual time

Fines

  • Base fine might be ~$390, but after court fees and assessments it's easily $2,000–$10,000 total

License Suspension

  • First offense: ~4–6 months (sometimes you can drive with restrictions)

DUI School

  • First offense: 3–9 months
  • Repeat offenders: up to 18–30 months

Ignition Interlock Device (IID)

  • You blow into it before your car will start
  • Becoming more common even for first offenses

Probation

Usually 3–5 years, with conditions like:

  • No driving with any alcohol in your system
  • Don't refuse chemical tests again
  • No new offenses

"Wet Reckless" — This Can Make a Big Difference

A wet reckless (California Vehicle Code 23103.5) is a reduced charge that a lawyer might be able to negotiate for a first-time case. Compared to a DUI conviction:

  • Lesser charge on your record
  • Lower fines
  • Shorter DUI school program
  • Less overall impact

The catch: it still counts as a prior DUI if you get another one later.

Things That Make It Worse (Even if Still a Misdemeanor)

Penalties can increase even without an injury if:

  • Your BAC was 0.15% or higher
  • You were speeding or driving recklessly
  • You refused a breath/blood test
  • There was a child in the car

Long-Term Consequences People Underestimate

  • Car insurance can skyrocket
  • Shows up on background checks
  • Can affect jobs — especially driving jobs
  • Stays on your driving record for 10 years
